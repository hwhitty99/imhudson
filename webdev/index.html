Web Development

I'm passionate about it

It all started in 1999. I was born. That's also when my mom started telling me that I should become and engineer. And rightly so. I liked solving problems, building things, taking things apart, fixing things, I was good at math...you get the picture. She had the best intentions and wanted to encourage me to pursue what I was good at and liked doing.

I didn't write a single line of code in highschool. So, when it came to picking a major, the only reason I even considered a CS degree was because I was technologically inclined and my favorite class up to that point had been my highschool's "technology" class (which was essentially Photoshop and Excel for beginners). I had rooted (and bricked and then revived) multiple Androids at that point and would spend ridiculous amounts of time designing my personal mobile software experience through launchers, icon packs, and custom ROMs. I was a UI/UX designer before I knew what that meant, but still thought I was for sure going to be an engineer.

So did I pick a CS degree or an engineering degree? Yes. Software Engineering.

Software Engineering at ONU, as a major, split ENGR and CSIS courses 50/50 and existed for maybe 6 years. Me and my 6 other software engineering majors were the last to graduate from ONU. I don't know what that says about the major but if it says anything it's probably not positive. The engineering half of my college education was arguably useless and the computer science half prepared me to work on microcontrollers and write java...which did not excite me.

What did excite me? For my year-long sophomore java course we programmed NXT robots to replicate the Simon game. I completed the project in record time. Then I spent about twice as long designing the monochromatic pixel art that displayed the player's level, their high score, and the navigation menu. That was not required work I just got really passionate about it for some reason. In my Android app development course, I discovered adaptive icons. Fun fact, on Andoird O and later, every app icon can be multiple layers and they react to your scolling in an elastic-like animation, which is so cool and satisfying. Don't believe me? Go try it. It's fun. I enjoyed creating those silly little icons more than actually programming the apps. Again, we weren't required to create adaptive icons for our apps and our prof never taught us about them; I was just passionate.

Finally, senior year rolled around. I needed some filler classes to be considered a full time student and be allowed to live on campus. I added a art/programming course to my schedule for my last semester of college. We developed a 2D scroller game, and 3D kart racing game in Unity, an app prototype in Adobe XD, and two websites: one to sell something that we could offer and one to display all our projects from the course. I was hooked! The game development didn't really speak to me, but I was incredibly excited about app prototyping and web development because they clicked for me. I understood the course material with ease and my projects were miles ahead of my classmates'. Granted, that wasn't a very high bar, but I'm still proud of what I created and it meant a lot to me at the time. I was the guy helping my classmates instead of the guy asking his classmates for help (which was usually the case).

The year prior to this class, I went home for spring break and never came back, due to COVID. During that whole "stay at home or you'll die and if you don't die you'll kill someone else" summer of 2020, I started an online clothing store through Shopify. I only mention this because it was a huge part in discovering my passion for web development/design. I loved designing each page of the website, even though it was tedious. I grew passionate about content layout, interaction animations, and user experience through this project. At this point, I still hadn't really written in any web development languages because I could do most of what I wanted through Shopify or their plugins. But, this was my first taste of HTML, CSS, and javascript because every once in a while they were required to get my vision for a page pixel perfect.

The rest really is history. Senior year I went deep down the web dev and UI design YouTube rabbit holes and, shortly after graduating, somehow got the job I'm at now where I'm still constantly learning and feel totally incompetnant next to my much more experienced coworkers.

    Some things I've learned and have used regularly since working at Mirus:
  • tailwindcss
  • alpine.js
  • handlebars.js
  • a11y
  • Vue
  • python
  • unit tests
  • django
  • Google Cloud Storage
  • GitLab
  • custom slackmojis

At this point I'm your average web developer who's obsessed with every new framework and plugin he sees but won't actually spend the time to learn/use them and I'm your average UI/UX designer who has a brilliant eye for design but feels like literally everyone else in the industry is better than me.

When I see a massive html file I get excited and want to figure out how the entire structure works. I've wasted time redesigning craiglist in the devTools. I watched the entire talk at Tailwind Connect on YouTube. I made my own VSCode theme. Half the reason I use Arc Browser is for the icon animations. I've spent time and money on two UI design courses taught by a YouTuber who owns a design agency. A decent percentage of my brain's storage is filled with keyboard shortcuts. I have a folder of bookmarks titled "things I want to use one day" that includes Pinia, Oruga, and Penpot. Just yesterday I was geeking out about this color contrast chart website and want to make a poster out of it.

These are just a few reasons why a senior developer I work with and respect once told me, "You have problems."

I love the internet and I want to improve as a web developer every day so that I can make it a more beautiful and useful place.
